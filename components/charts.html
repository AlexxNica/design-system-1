Chart BasicsIn Progress

When considering using a data visualization in your project, always value substance over flash. The most important thing is making sure it is easy to interpret the data and glean insights. Charts can also present accessiblity chanlenges designers must address. Remember to keep in mind color blind users, keyboard accessibility, and users with screen readers.

Best Practices

  • Title the chart and label axises. The only exception to labeling axises is when the x-axis shows time. Don't make the user guess what data points mean.
  • Always use a legand when color is used to convey meaning in a chart.
  • Display a threshold or average line when the information is available. This gives the user quick context as to whether the data point requires special attention and additional investigation.
  • Verify the information in the charts is also available to someone using a screen reader.
  • Never fully rely on hover tooltips to convey information.

Use of Color

Color should be only be used intentially to differentiate between data in charts. When using color different colors in a chart, always show a legend. Avoid using more than seven colors in a single chart. It will become to difficult for users to distinguish between them.

Color blindness affects 8% of men and 0.5% of women worldwide.

Bar ChartIn Progress

Use to compare items against each other or throughout time and identify outliers.

Bar charts showing a single dataset should use a single color, and there is no need to show a legend.

When comparing throughout time, bar charts and line charts can sometimes be used interchangably. Use a line chart if:

  • The bar chart showing one dataset would have more than 15 bars or multi-dataset bar chart will have more than 24 bars.
  • Small or barely distiguishable differences are expected between bar heights. Small differences will be easier to identify in a line chart.

Multiple Datasets

Bar charts can also compare up to four datasets against eachother over time. Distinguish between datasets with color and show a legend. Assure the same y-axis unit applies to each dataset. Datasets should also have similar orders of magitude. For example, changes in the annual discharge of a local creek will be unnoticable if displayed on the same chart as the Nile River's annual discarge.

Use to show relationship between variables or show more than two variables at once.

A bubble chart displays three variables of data at a time. It can show the correlation between the x- and y-axis variables. A third variable determines the size of the bubble. Be conscientious of the number of bubbles and the size of the chart. Too many bubbles on too small of a chart can create a useless visualization.

A legend doesn't necessarily need to be shown with this chart. However, it can be used to filter what bubbles are displayed and therefore helpful in some circumstances. Make sure to include a custom scale indicating what bubble diameter indicates.

Line ChartIn Progress

Use: trends over time

Much of the guidence around bar charts also applies to line charts. Line charts with a single line do not need a legend. Up to six lines can be displayed on a single chart with a legend. Lines must be throroughly differentiated with color or pattern. The same y-axis unit must apply to all lines on the chart.

Pie and doughnut charts show composition and are the only Helix charts that show an item's share of the total. Limit the number of slices to seven or less. If there are more categories than that, group all but the largest six into an other category. Always show legends with a pie chart.

Polar Area ChartIn Progress

Use to identify outliers and display cyclical data.

Polar area charts have similarities to both bar and pie charts. Unlike pie charts, they do not show an item's share of the total. Instead each wedge has an equal angle. The wedges extend from the center to show deviations in the data the way bars extend from the x-axis in the above bar chart examples. Polar area charts are best at showing cyclical data.

Since polar area charts are less common compared to bar and pie charts, gleaning insights from them can increase cognitive load. Depending on your data, consider showing a bar chart or pie chart instead.
